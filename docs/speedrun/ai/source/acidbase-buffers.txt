Acids, Bases, and Buffers
Source: OpenStax, Chemistry 2e (CC BY 4.0) — https://openstax.org/books/chemistry-2e — adapted.

Brønsted-Lowry acids and bases are defined in terms of proton transfer. An acid
is a species that donates a proton (H+); a base is a species that accepts a
proton. When an acid donates a proton, the remaining species is its conjugate
base; when a base accepts a proton, it becomes its conjugate acid. In the
reaction between water and ammonia, water donates a proton to ammonia, forming
hydroxide (the conjugate base of water) and ammonium (the conjugate acid of
ammonia). Species that can either donate or accept a proton are amphiprotic;
water is the classic example.

Water undergoes autoionization: 2 H2O ⇌ H3O+ + OH-, quantified by the
ion-product constant Kw = [H3O+][OH-]. At 25 °C, Kw = 1.0 × 10^-14, and in pure
water [H3O+] = [OH-] = 1.0 × 10^-7 M. This inverse relationship between hydronium
and hydroxide holds for all aqueous solutions at a given temperature.

A buffer solution contains appreciable amounts of a weak conjugate acid-base
pair (for example acetic acid with sodium acetate, or ammonia with ammonium
chloride) and resists changes in pH when small amounts of strong acid or base
are added. Added strong base is neutralized by the weak acid; added strong acid
is neutralized by the conjugate base. Because the weak acid and base ionize only
slightly, the pH changes far less than it would in an unbuffered solution.

The pH of a buffer is given by the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation, derived from
the acid ionization constant Ka = [H3O+][A-]/[HA]:

    pH = pKa + log([A-]/[HA])

When the concentrations of the conjugate base and weak acid are equal, pH = pKa.
Buffer capacity is the amount of acid or base a buffer can absorb before its pH
changes by about one unit; it increases with the absolute concentrations of the
two components. A buffer works best when the two components are present in
roughly equal amounts, and it loses effectiveness once one component drops below
about 10% of the other.
