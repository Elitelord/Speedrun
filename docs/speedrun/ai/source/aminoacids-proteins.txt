Amino Acids and Protein Structure
Source: Wikipedia, "Amino acid" (CC BY-SA 4.0) — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid — adapted.

An amino acid has a central (alpha) carbon bonded to four groups: an amino group
(NH2), a carboxylic acid group (COOH), a hydrogen atom, and a variable side chain
(R group). The R group determines the amino acid's chemistry. Side chains are
classified as nonpolar/hydrophobic (e.g. alanine, valine, leucine, isoleucine,
methionine, phenylalanine, tryptophan, proline), polar uncharged (serine,
threonine, asparagine, glutamine), acidic/negatively charged at physiological pH
(aspartate, glutamate), or basic/positively charged (arginine, lysine, and
histidine). Histidine has a side-chain pKa near 6.0, so it exists in both charged
and uncharged forms near physiological pH, which makes it valuable in enzyme
acid-base catalysis.

Near neutral pH an amino acid exists as a zwitterion, with a protonated amino
group (NH3+) and a deprotonated carboxyl group (COO-): NH3+-CHR-COO-. Net charge
is zero but the molecule carries both a positive and a negative site. In strongly
acidic solution (low pH) the carboxylate is protonated (NH3+-CHR-COOH, net +); in
strongly basic solution (high pH) the ammonium loses its proton (NH2-CHR-COO-,
net -).

The isoelectric point (pI) is the pH at which the molecule carries no net charge.
For an amino acid with a nonionizable side chain, pI = ½(pKa1 + pKa2). For one
with an ionizable side chain, use the two pKa values that bracket the neutral
species (e.g. for an acidic amino acid, pI = ½(pKa1 + pKa,R)). Net charge depends
on pH relative to pI: below the pI the molecule is net positive, above the pI it
is net negative, and at the pI charges balance (zero electrophoretic mobility,
minimum solubility).

Amino acids join by peptide bonds — amide linkages formed by a condensation
(dehydration) reaction between the carboxyl group of one amino acid and the amino
group of the next, releasing water. The chain (a polypeptide) runs from the
N-terminus to the C-terminus. Protein structure has four levels: primary (the
linear sequence), secondary (local folds such as the alpha helix and beta
pleated sheet, stabilized by backbone hydrogen bonds), tertiary (the overall 3-D
fold of one chain, stabilized by side-chain interactions — hydrophobic contacts,
hydrogen bonds, ionic/salt bridges, and disulfide bonds between cysteines), and
quaternary (assembly of multiple subunits, as in hemoglobin).
