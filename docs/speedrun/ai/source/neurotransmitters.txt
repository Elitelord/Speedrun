Neurotransmitters and Synaptic Transmission
Source: Wikipedia, "Neurotransmitter" (CC BY-SA 4.0) — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neurotransmitter — adapted.

A neurotransmitter is a signaling molecule released by a neuron to affect another
cell across a synapse. When an action potential reaches the axon terminal,
voltage-gated calcium channels open, Ca2+ enters, and synaptic vesicles fuse with
the presynaptic membrane to release neurotransmitter into the synaptic cleft. The
transmitter diffuses across the cleft and binds receptors on the postsynaptic
cell; the receptor determines whether the effect is excitatory (raises the chance
of a postsynaptic action potential), inhibitory (lowers it), or modulatory.

Major neurotransmitters:
  • Glutamate — the main excitatory transmitter (>90% of synapses); central to
    learning and synaptic plasticity; excess causes excitotoxicity.
  • GABA — the main inhibitory transmitter; target of many sedatives/anxiolytics.
  • Dopamine — reward, motivation, and fine motor control; loss in the substantia
    nigra causes Parkinson's; excess signaling is linked to schizophrenia.
  • Serotonin — mood, sleep, appetite, and more; implicated in depression.
  • Acetylcholine — the first transmitter discovered; acts at the neuromuscular
    junction (activating skeletal muscle) and widely in the brain.
  • Norepinephrine — a catecholamine (from tyrosine); fight-or-flight arousal,
    attention, mood.

Signaling is terminated so receptors are not activated continuously, by three
mechanisms: diffusion out of the cleft (with uptake by glia), enzymatic
degradation (e.g. acetylcholinesterase breaks down acetylcholine), and reuptake
(transporter proteins pump the transmitter back into the presynaptic neuron).
These steps are drug targets — cocaine, for example, blocks dopamine reuptake so
dopamine accumulates in the cleft.
