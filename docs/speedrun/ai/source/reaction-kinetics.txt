Chemical Kinetics: Rate Laws
Source: OpenStax, Chemistry 2e (CC BY 4.0) — https://openstax.org/books/chemistry-2e — adapted.

The rate of a chemical reaction generally depends on the concentrations of its
reactants. A rate law (differential rate equation) expresses this relationship.
For a general reaction aA + bB → products, the rate law has the form:

    rate = k[A]^m[B]^n

Here [A] and [B] are molar concentrations, k is the rate constant (specific to a
reaction at a given temperature), and the exponents m and n are the reaction
orders. The rate constant k is independent of reactant concentrations but does
vary with temperature.

The reaction orders m and n must be determined experimentally and are frequently
different from the stoichiometric coefficients in the balanced equation. They are
typically positive integers but can be fractional, negative, or zero. The
reaction is m-order in A and n-order in B; the overall order is the sum m + n.
The order sets the mathematical dependence of rate on concentration: for a
first-order dependence, doubling that reactant's concentration doubles the rate;
for a second-order dependence, doubling it quadruples the rate; for zero order,
changing the concentration does not change the rate.

The units of k depend on the overall reaction order: zero order, mol·L^-1·s^-1;
first order, s^-1; second order, L·mol^-1·s^-1; third order, L^2·mol^-2·s^-1.
