Thermodynamics and Bioenergetics

The first law of thermodynamics states that energy is conserved: it can be
transformed but not created or destroyed. The internal energy of a system changes
by the heat added to it minus the work it does on the surroundings.

The second law states that the total entropy of an isolated system tends to
increase. Entropy is a measure of the dispersal of energy and the number of
accessible microstates; spontaneous processes increase the entropy of the
universe.

Gibbs free energy combines these ideas for processes at constant temperature and
pressure. The change in Gibbs free energy equals the change in enthalpy minus the
temperature times the change in entropy. A reaction is spontaneous (exergonic)
when the free energy change is negative, and nonspontaneous (endergonic) when it
is positive. When the free energy change is zero the reaction is at equilibrium.

Enthalpy is the heat content at constant pressure. An exothermic reaction
releases heat and has a negative enthalpy change, while an endothermic reaction
absorbs heat and has a positive enthalpy change.

In cells, endergonic reactions are driven by coupling them to the exergonic
hydrolysis of ATP. The hydrolysis of ATP to ADP and inorganic phosphate releases
free energy that powers cellular work.
