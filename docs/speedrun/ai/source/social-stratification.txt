Social Stratification
Source: Wikipedia, "Social stratification" (CC BY-SA 4.0) — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_stratification — adapted.

Social stratification is a society's hierarchical ranking of people into groups
based on socioeconomic factors such as wealth, income, education, occupation,
race, ethnicity, and social status. It is a property of society (not of
individuals), is reproduced across generations, and varies across time and place.

Systems of stratification:
  • Class systems (typical of modern Western societies) divide people into upper,
    middle, and working classes and are relatively open — position depends partly
    on achieved status.
  • Caste systems are closed — status is ascribed at birth and fixed, with little
    mobility.

Social mobility is movement between strata: intragenerational (within a person's
lifetime) or intergenerational (across generations). Open systems permit mobility
based on achievement; closed systems (castes) permit little.

Major theoretical perspectives:
  • Marx: class is defined by one's relationship to the means of production —
    owners vs. workers who sell their labor; he predicted class conflict.
  • Weber: stratification has three independent dimensions — class (economic
    position), status (prestige/honor), and power — so wealth, prestige, and
    power need not coincide.
  • Structural functionalism (Davis-Moore hypothesis): inequality is functional
    because more demanding positions that require scarce training are rewarded
    more to motivate people to fill them.

Stratification variables extend beyond economics to gender, race/ethnicity, age,
education, and geography. At the global scale, world-systems theory distinguishes
core, semi-peripheral, and peripheral nations, mirroring domestic class
hierarchies.
