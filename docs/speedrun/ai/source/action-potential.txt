Neurons and the Action Potential
Source: Wikipedia, "Action potential" (CC BY-SA 4.0) — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_potential — adapted.

A neuron maintains a resting membrane potential of about -70 mV, with the inside
negative relative to the outside. This results from ion pumps and channels;
potassium leaking out down its concentration gradient is a major contributor.

When input depolarizes the membrane (makes it less negative) to the threshold
(about -55 mV), the neuron fires. Action potentials depend on voltage-gated ion
channels. Above threshold, voltage-gated sodium channels open rapidly and Na+
(more concentrated outside) rushes in, making the interior more positive; this
opens still more sodium channels — a positive-feedback loop that drives the fast
rising phase (depolarization).

At the peak, two things happen: sodium channels inactivate (the same voltage that
opened them now closes them), and slower voltage-gated potassium channels open,
letting K+ flow outward. The shift from sodium to potassium current drives the
falling phase (repolarization). Because some potassium channels stay open a bit
too long, the membrane briefly overshoots to more negative than rest
(hyperpolarization / afterhyperpolarization) before returning to the resting
potential.

Action potentials are all-or-none: either threshold is reached and a full,
constant-amplitude spike fires, or nothing happens. Stimulus strength is encoded
by firing frequency, not amplitude. After a spike, the absolute refractory period
(inactivated sodium channels cannot reopen, ~1 ms) makes another spike
impossible; the following relative refractory period (lingering K+ efflux
hyperpolarizes the cell) allows firing only with a stronger stimulus. The
absolute refractory period ensures the action potential propagates in one
direction along the axon.
