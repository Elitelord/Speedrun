Neurons and the Action Potential

A neuron maintains a resting membrane potential of about negative seventy
millivolts, with the inside of the cell negative relative to the outside. This
potential is established by the sodium-potassium pump, which moves three sodium
ions out of the cell for every two potassium ions it moves in, and by the
membrane's selective permeability to potassium.

An action potential is a rapid, all-or-none change in membrane potential that
travels along the axon. When a stimulus depolarizes the membrane to threshold,
voltage-gated sodium channels open and sodium rushes into the cell, driving the
membrane potential sharply positive. This is depolarization.

Repolarization follows as the sodium channels inactivate and voltage-gated
potassium channels open, allowing potassium to leave the cell. The membrane
briefly becomes more negative than the resting potential, a state called
hyperpolarization, before returning to rest.

During the refractory period the neuron cannot fire a second action potential,
which ensures the signal travels in one direction. In myelinated axons the
signal jumps between the nodes of Ranvier in a process called saltatory
conduction, which greatly increases conduction speed.

When the action potential reaches the axon terminal it triggers the release of
neurotransmitters into the synapse, carrying the signal to the next cell.
