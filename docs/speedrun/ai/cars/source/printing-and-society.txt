The spread of the printing press in fifteenth-century Europe is often described
as a sudden revolution, but its effects were slower and stranger than that image
suggests. Movable type did not create the appetite for books; that appetite
already existed among merchants, clergy, and a growing class of literate
townspeople. What the press changed was scale. A manuscript that once took a
scribe months to copy could now be produced in hundreds of identical copies,
and identical copies made a new kind of trust possible: a reader in one city
could be confident that the text before them matched the one discussed elsewhere.

This standardization had consequences no one intended. When errors could be
compared across copies, they could also be corrected, and later editions grew
more accurate rather than drifting apart as hand-copied texts had. Fixed page
numbers and indexes turned books into tools that could be searched, not merely
read from beginning to end. Knowledge became cumulative in a way that favored
those who could gather many books and cross-reference them.

Yet the same technology that spread reliable information also spread error and
propaganda with equal efficiency. Printers were businesses, and they printed what
sold: sensational pamphlets, dubious remedies, and polemics as readily as
scripture and science. Authorities that had once controlled a small number of
scribes now faced a diffuse network of presses they could not easily monitor, and
their attempts at censorship often advertised the very works they hoped to
suppress.

Historians caution against crediting the press with outcomes that depended on many
forces at once. The religious and political upheavals of the following century
drew on economic strain, dynastic ambition, and long-standing grievances; print
amplified these currents rather than causing them. The lesson, some argue, is
that a communication technology rarely dictates a single result. It lowers the
cost of copying and distribution, and in doing so it strengthens whatever
tendencies — toward inquiry or toward manipulation — are already present in a
society. The press was powerful precisely because it was neutral about what it
carried.
