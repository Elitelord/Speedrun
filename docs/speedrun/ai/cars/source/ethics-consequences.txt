Moral philosophers have long disagreed about what makes an action right. One
tradition, consequentialism, holds that the rightness of an act depends entirely
on its outcomes. In its best-known form, utilitarianism, the right action is the
one that produces the greatest balance of well-being over suffering, counting
every affected person's interests equally. On this view a policy is justified not
by the intentions behind it but by the aggregate good it brings about, and a
seemingly harsh choice can be defensible if it prevents a larger harm.

A rival tradition, often associated with Kant, locates rightness not in
consequences but in duties and the respect owed to persons. Here some acts are
wrong in themselves, regardless of the good they might produce, because they
treat a person merely as a means to an end. Lying, breaking a promise, or
sacrificing an innocent to benefit others violates a duty that no favorable
outcome can cancel. Duty-based ethics prizes consistency: a principle is
acceptable only if one could will that everyone follow it.

Critics of consequentialism argue that it can license troubling acts whenever the
numbers favor them, and that it demands more calculation than people can perform
in real time. Critics of duty-based ethics reply that rigid rules can produce
avoidable misery, as when honesty toward a murderer endangers an innocent life.
Each view, in other words, is most vulnerable precisely where the other is
strongest: consequentialism struggles to protect the individual against the
group, while duty ethics struggles to bend when the stakes are extreme.

Some thinkers seek a middle path. Rule consequentialism asks not which act but
which general rule would, if widely adopted, produce the best outcomes, thereby
preserving stable duties like promise-keeping while still grounding them in human
welfare. Others argue that the two traditions are answering different questions —
one about what to do, the other about what kind of person to be — and that a
mature moral life draws on both. What unites the debate is a shared refusal to
reduce ethics to mere preference: whatever the right answer, these theories
insist that moral claims can be reasoned about, defended, and revised.
