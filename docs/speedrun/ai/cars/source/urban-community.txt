Early sociologists worried that the modern city would dissolve community. In the
village, they argued, people knew one another across many roles — a neighbor was
also a customer, a fellow worshipper, a relative — and these overlapping ties
bound behavior tightly. The city seemed to replace such thick relationships with
thin ones: crowds of strangers, brief transactions, and a freedom that could
shade into isolation. On this account urban life traded belonging for liberty,
and the price of privacy was loneliness.

Later research complicated the picture. Studies of actual city neighborhoods
found that residents did not live among undifferentiated strangers but built
dense networks within them — on a block, in a congregation, around a shared
language or trade. The city did not abolish community so much as let people choose
it. Freed from the obligation to belong to whatever group they were born into,
urban dwellers assembled communities of affinity, gathering with others who shared
their interests even when those others lived across town.

This selectivity carried its own costs. Communities of choice can be tightly knit
yet narrow, reinforcing the views their members already hold and offering little
contact with people unlike themselves. The very anonymity that lets a person
escape a stifling hometown also lets a city fragment into enclaves that rarely
meet. Whether this counts as the loss of community or its reinvention depends on
what one thinks community is for: mutual surveillance and shared fate, or support
and self-expression.

The debate matters beyond nostalgia. If cities weaken the accidental ties that put
different people in regular contact, then institutions that once did that work —
public schools, markets, transit, parks — take on new importance as the last
places where strangers must accommodate one another. Some scholars therefore argue
that the health of urban community depends less on how many friends a person has
than on how often the design of a city forces unlike people to share space. On
this view community is not only something one feels but something a place either
affords or forecloses.
